# TaxTheBot — Vision

## What This Is

TaxTheBot is an advocacy data platform that tracks AI-generated corporate profits, quantifies workforce displacement, and makes the case for robot taxation as a transition-funding mechanism.

The core claim: AI companies are capturing unprecedented profits while displacing workers at scale, and a dedicated tax on AI-derived revenue could fund retraining, UBI, and community investment for affected populations.

## The Honest State of Things

Today, TaxTheBot is a well-designed landing page with hardcoded data. It is not yet a data platform. The "LIVE DATA" badge, ticking counters, and per-second rates are cosmetic — every number is a hand-written estimate in a TypeScript file. The newsletter doesn't connect to anything. The share buttons don't link out. The letter template is a dead end.

This is the gap between what the site *presents itself as* and what it *actually is*. Closing that gap is the single most important thing that has to happen.

## Thesis: Why Robot Taxes, Why Now

### The Window

The argument for AI-specific taxation is strongest in the 2025-2032 window:

1. **Displacement is visible but unaddressed.** Companies are reporting record AI-driven profits in the same quarters they announce layoffs. The juxtaposition is stark and politically potent.
2. **The tax base is eroding.** Government payroll tax revenue depends on humans having jobs. Every automated task that replaces a worker removes a taxpayer without removing a citizen who needs services.
3. **Concentration creates a target.** AI profits are concentrating in 5-8 identifiable companies. This is politically simpler to tax than diffuse economic activity.
4. **Regulatory momentum exists.** The EU AI Act, proposed US disclosure requirements, and state-level automation impact assessments are creating a legislative environment where AI-specific policy is thinkable.

### The Counterarguments (and Why They Don't Fully Land)

A credible platform must reckon with the strongest objections:

**"AI creates more jobs than it destroys."**
Historical technology waves have generated net employment gains. This may be true again. But the *transition period* is what matters — the decade between displacement and new job creation devastates specific communities even if the macro trend eventually recovers. Robot taxes fund the bridge, not a permanent state.

**"You can't define 'AI revenue.'"**
This is the strongest technical objection. As AI becomes embedded in every product, isolating "AI-derived" revenue becomes impossible. The response: start with clear cases (autonomous coding tools, AI-generated content platforms, automated customer service) and let the definition evolve with the technology, as tax policy always does.

**"Broader wealth or corporate taxes are better."**
Possibly. But they aren't happening, and they don't create the right incentives. A displacement-linked tax specifically incentivizes companies to keep humans in the loop. A generic corporate tax does not.

**"Companies will offshore."**
The same argument was made against every corporate tax ever. Some will. Most won't, because the US market is too large to abandon. And international coordination (as with the OECD minimum tax) addresses the race-to-the-bottom concern.

## Business Model Options

TaxTheBot doesn't have to monetize. But it does have to be *durable* — funded, maintained, and authoritative over years, not just at launch. Possible models:

### A. Policy Think Tank (most natural fit)
Operate as a 501(c)(3) or fiscal-sponsored project. Revenue from grants (Ford Foundation, Omidyar Network, Open Society), donations, and event fees. The data platform, reports, and policy analysis are the product. **Model:** Economic Policy Institute, Tax Foundation, Brookings.

### B. Data Intelligence Service
Maintain a proprietary dataset that tracks AI profits vs. displacement in real time. Free tier is the public site. Paid tier sells detailed reports, API access, and custom analysis to: labor unions, government agencies, journalists, academic researchers. **Model:** PitchBook, CB Insights.

### C. Advocacy Media
Build an audience through regular content — weekly newsletter, quarterly reports, annual "State of AI Displacement" publication. Monetize through subscriptions or grants. **Model:** The Markup, ProPublica.

### D. Policy Consulting
Municipalities and states considering AI impact legislation hire TaxTheBot for impact assessments and tax model recommendations. **Model:** Urban Institute, RAND (for government clients).

**Recommended starting point:** A hybrid of A + C. Build the data credibility first (think tank). Distribute it through media (newsletter, social, annual report). This creates the brand authority needed before any other model is viable.

## What Makes This Irreplaceable

When AI commoditizes everything — when anyone can generate a pretty landing page with fake data in 5 minutes — what does TaxTheBot have that can't be replicated?

### 1. Verified, Continuously Updated Data
A pipeline that actually ingests SEC filings, BLS employment data, LinkedIn layoff signals, and earnings transcripts — then computes novel metrics like "AI profit per displaced worker." This is the core asset. Mock data is worthless. Real, maintained, verifiable data is the thing no one else is building.

### 2. The "AI Profit per Displaced Worker" Metric
This is TaxTheBot's potential signature contribution. No one else is computing this. If the methodology is rigorous enough to be cited by economists and policymakers, it becomes a standard — like how the Gini coefficient frames inequality discussions. Owning the metric owns the frame.

### 3. Brand Ownership of the Discourse
"Robot tax" is entering mainstream policy vocabulary. TaxTheBot is a memorable, shareable brand that could own that search term. In advocacy, being the default citation is an enormous compounding advantage. First-mover on the frame matters.

### 4. A Real Coalition
If TaxTheBot builds an actual constituency — displaced workers who share their stories, labor unions that cite the data, legislators who reference it in committee hearings — that network is the ultimate moat. Political organizing compounds. AI can generate websites; it cannot generate trust or political power.

## Weakest Assumptions, Ranked

1. **Data credibility.** Mock data on a "data platform" is a foundational contradiction. The site's visual language promises rigor it doesn't deliver. This must be fixed first, or nothing else matters.

2. **Theory of change is missing.** "Take action" with no strategy is performance. What happens after someone copies the letter? Who receives it? What's the legislative pathway? Which committee, which bill, which session? Without this, the site is venting, not organizing.

3. **One-sided framing limits the audience.** The people who already agree don't need convincing. The people who make policy need to see that TaxTheBot has reckoned with counterarguments. Without intellectual honesty, the site reads as propaganda to the exact audience it needs to persuade.

4. **No feedback loop.** Zero analytics, no working newsletter, no engagement tracking. There's no way to know if anyone cares, what resonates, or whether the site is having any impact at all.

5. **Static content in a dynamic field.** AI economics change quarterly. A site that never updates its data or analysis dies. What's the update cadence? Who maintains it?

## The Strategic Sequence

If this is going to be durable, the order matters:

**Phase 1: Credibility** (immediate)
- Replace mock data with sourced, verifiable numbers (even if manually curated from public sources)
- Add methodology page explaining how each number was calculated
- Add source citations to every data point
- Remove "LIVE DATA" badge or make it actually live
- Add counterarguments section to build intellectual credibility

**Phase 2: Distribution** (next)
- Launch real newsletter (even simple — Buttondown, Substack)
- Publish a "State of AI Displacement" report (quarterly or annual)
- Create embeddable widgets other sites can use (share the data)
- Connect share buttons to actual sharing functionality

**Phase 3: Coalition** (ongoing)
- Partner with labor organizations, displaced worker communities
- Build a legislative tracker (which bills, which committees, what status)
- Collect and publish displacement stories from real people
- Get cited — pitch to journalists, researchers, policymakers

**Phase 4: Automation** (later)
- Build the data pipeline: SEC EDGAR for filings, BLS API for employment, NLP on earnings calls for AI revenue mentions
- Automate the novel metrics
- Offer API access for researchers

## The Question This All Comes Down To

Is TaxTheBot a *website* or a *movement*?

If it's a website, it already works. Ship it, share it, move on.

If it's a movement, the website is the least important part. The data pipeline, the methodology, the coalition, the legislative strategy — those are what make it durable. The site is just the front door.

The answer to that question determines everything that comes next.
